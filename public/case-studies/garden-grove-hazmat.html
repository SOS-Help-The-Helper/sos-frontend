What This Document Is

This document catalogs the information failures observed during the Garden Grove hazmat incident and measures them against two frameworks:

Part 1 — APCO / NIMS / ADA Standards
Measures the incident against APCO ANS 1.121.1-2022, NIMS/ICS Joint Information System requirements, FEMA IPAWS/CAP, ADA Title II, and Executive Order 13166 (LEP access). Documents 13 specific failure modes with evidence.

Part 2 — FEMA Key Planning Factors (Bio KPF 2.2)
Measures the incident against FEMA's Key Planning Factors for a Biological Incident, KPF 2.2: Communications for an Informed Public. While written for biological incidents, the public communication principles are universal. Documents 14 FEMA principles — 0 of 14 fully met.

Purpose: Standards evaluation only. This document does not recommend solutions, propose features, or reference any specific technology platform. It documents what went wrong and measures it against published emergency communications standards.

Problem Statement 1

Evacuation Maps Were Illegible at Street Level

What Happened

Seven documented citizen complaints that evacuation zone maps posted by agencies were unreadable at street level. Maps were shared as static PNG/JPEG images on social media — no zoom, no interactivity, no street-level detail. Citizens could not determine whether their specific address was inside or outside the evacuation zone.

  • Elizabeth Cameron (Facebook): Cannot see street-level details on posted maps
  • Yessi Vega (Facebook): Maps "not clear enough to read street names"
  • Dana Booth (Facebook): Developed citizen workaround — cross-referencing center junction of official map on Google Maps
  • City of Stanton: Issued an official "Map Clarification" graphic, confirming original maps caused confusion
  • City of Westminster: Admitted zone borders require a "literal line drawn" and encouraged citizens near borders to "self-evacuate if feeling unsafe"

"I'm trying to find a good map but every one I open has me having to download another app. WTF people? I don't need a new app to see a map!!"

E. Locke (@SandSteelCotton, Day 3 — 72+ hours into the incident)

What the Standard Says

APCO 1.121.1-2022, §1.1.3.1.3: "Coordinate with agency representatives or PIOs to keep the public informed"
APCO 1.121.1-2022, §4.1.4.3: ECCs "should create dashboards, visualization tools, and other platforms that support shared situational awareness"
FEMA IPAWS: Alerts must include "a description of the affected area" with sufficient geographic specificity for citizens to determine personal risk

The Gap

The standard requires public information sufficient for citizens to act. Maps shared during this incident required citizens to develop their own workarounds (cross-referencing on Google Maps) to answer the basic question: "Am I in the zone?" Two cities officially acknowledged their maps were inadequate. Map illegibility was not a first-24-hour problem — it continued across the entire incident.

Problem Statement 2

No Single Source of Truth Existed Across 6 Jurisdictions

What Happened

Six jurisdictions (Garden Grove, Westminster, Stanton, Cypress, Anaheim, Buena Park) plus the county, state agencies, and the lead agency (OCFA) all published information independently. No single source aggregated evacuation zones, shelter locations, road closures, and resources across jurisdictions.

  • Garden Grove built an interactive address lookup — only covers their city
  • Anaheim built a separate ArcGIS lookup — different tool, different data
  • Westminster linked to OC Sheriff's generic pre-disaster page
  • Buena Park posted a text banner with no map
  • Cypress told residents via Mailchimp newsletter to "follow OCFA on X" — zero shelter info
  • OCFA (lead agency) had no website presence — X/Twitter only
  • Governor's Linktree linked to 5 city websites that each linked back to the same generic county resources

What the Standard Says

APCO 1.121.1-2022, §2.1.3.5.2: "ECCs shall have interoperability agreements with surrounding agencies"
NIMS/ICS: Unified Command requires coordinated public information through a Joint Information Center (JIC) or Joint Information System (JIS)
FEMA IPAWS/CAP: Designed to provide "a simple but general format for exchanging all-hazard emergency alerts" — interoperability is the foundational premise

The Gap

Despite APCO, NIMS, and CAP all requiring cross-agency coordination, six jurisdictions published independently with no cross-reference, no aggregation, and no coordination. A citizen in the evacuation zone would need to check 7+ websites and social media accounts to assemble a complete picture.

Why This Matters

FEMA's Own Standards vs. FEMA's Own Incident

This is not an obscure standard. This is FEMA's own published framework for how to communicate with the public during a CBRN incident — the exact category of event occurring in Garden Grove.

The Garden Grove incident, while activating a FEMA Region 9 response, triggering a Governor's emergency declaration, involving an EPA on-scene coordinator, and prompting IMAAC plume modeling, failed to meet FEMA's own public communication standards on 12 of 14 dimensions.

The failures are systemic: no cross-jurisdiction information system, no pre-identified public messaging infrastructure, no mechanism for aggregating resources across agencies, and no way for citizens to receive consistent, accessible, cumulative information regardless of which jurisdiction they happen to live in.
