Community-led coordination.
Not another government system.

The first 72 hours of a disaster are the most critical and the least coordinated. Formal systems take days to activate. The people doing life-saving work in those hours are community members with no tools and no institutional support.

SOS doesn't wait for a federal declaration. It doesn't ask communities to adopt a new platform. It shows up in the tools they already use — WhatsApp, Slack, iMessage, Facebook — and gives them a coordination layer underneath.

The 72-Hour Gap

FEMA's own SAR tool requires internet connectivity. Communities in the first 72 hours often have none. SOS is designed for the gap between when the disaster hits and when the institutions arrive — because that's when the most lives are saved and lost.

Everyone is a helper.

There is no survivor/helper binary. Needs and offers coexist on the same person. A person who receives help in the morning can provide it in the afternoon.

A displaced family with a truck can clear roads for their neighbors.

A retired nurse in a flood zone can triage injuries while waiting for her own evacuation.

A teenager who speaks three languages can run comms for families who don't speak English.

A rancher 500 miles away has a semi-truck and 20 tons of hay — and wants to help.

The public isn't a liability. They're the first responder.
