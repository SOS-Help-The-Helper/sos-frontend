The Crisis

The Great Aid Recession

For the first time in modern history, the global humanitarian system is contracting — not because disasters are slowing down, but because funding, infrastructure, and political will are collapsing simultaneously. FEMA has stopped funding non-lifesaving disaster recovery.⁴ Twenty states have sued to restart the largest federal preparedness grant program. Global humanitarian funding dropped 40% in a single year² — the largest shortfall ever recorded.

The Problem

When coordination fails, help scatters.

The help always shows up. After Hurricane Helene, FEMA's system crashed under ten times its normal volume.³ Search and rescue checked the same homes three times. Facebook became the missing persons database — not by design, but because nothing else worked. Volunteers converge on visible damage while isolated communities go unserved. This pattern repeats every disaster, everywhere.

When coordination fails

Three organizations can help one family. Without coordination, two never hear about the need. The third shows up a week late. The family called nine hotlines. Nobody tracks what was spent.

When coordination works

The shelter, food team, and medical transport all receive the same match simultaneously. Each knows what the other is doing. Every dollar traced. Every contribution attributed. The system learns.

The technology to make this possible didn't exist two years ago. It does now.

The Solution

Relief as permaculture.

SOS is coordination infrastructure that connects citizens, nonprofits, government agencies, emergency services, and vendors — so that when someone needs help and someone else can provide it, the connection actually happens.

The system doesn't just respond to disasters — it learns from them. Revenue from connecting homeowners with vetted contractors funds coordination for people with no insurance, no savings, no safety net.

SOS started during Hurricane Helene. Our founder's farmhouse was damaged. He lived in a donated RV for three months.

Since Helene, SOS has activated for every major disaster — including the Blizzard of 2026,¹⁰ coordinating hundreds of resources across 12 states.

The values are structural, not aspirational. Partners keep their data. Citizens control their privacy. No profiles. No surveillance. Communities deserve infrastructure that works for them, not on them.

A displaced family finds temporary housing in a stranger's guest room — then clears roads with their truck by morning.

A nurse, still in scrubs, triages her neighbors on the front porch — while her own home floods behind her.

A teenager who speaks three languages becomes the only translator for miles — because no agency thought to plan for it.

Needs and offers coexist on the same person, sometimes on the same day. The instinct is already there. What they lack isn't motivation — it's infrastructure.
