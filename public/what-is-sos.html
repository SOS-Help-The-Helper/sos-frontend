The Crisis

The disaster within the disaster.

Every major disaster in modern history has exposed the same failure — not in the people who respond, but in the systems that are supposed to coordinate them.

After Hurricane Helene hit western North Carolina in September 2024, FEMA's resource tracking system crashed under ten times its normal volume. Search and rescue teams checked the same homes three and four times because two platforms didn't talk to each other.¹ Facebook became the missing persons database — not by design, but because nothing else worked. Volunteers were turned away at the edge of the damage zone because nobody could tell them where to bring it.

This pattern isn't new. From Katrina to Sandy, Harvey to Maria, the same coordination failures surface every time. The tools break. The data fragments. The people who want to help can't find the people who need it.

Federal Safety Net

The net is contracting

As of April 2026, FEMA has stopped funding non-lifesaving disaster recovery.² Twenty states have sued to restart the largest federal preparedness grant program.³ GAO added disaster assistance to its high-risk list for the first time.⁴

Global Humanitarian Crisis

The Great Aid Recession

305 million people needed humanitarian assistance in 2025. 207 million didn't receive it.⁵ The $24.4 billion funding shortfall is the largest on record.⁶ Humanitarian funding dropped 40 percent in a single year.⁷

Coordination Failure

Volunteer convergence

After Helene, 54 fire departments, hundreds of nonprofits, and thousands of volunteers responded — across 19 different systems with zero shared visibility. Researchers call this "volunteer convergence": the secondary disaster within the disaster.

The Help Exists

Everyone responds.
No one can see each other.

The response to every disaster is bigger, faster, and more capable than any system gives it credit for.

Citizens

The actual first responders

Three out of four people turn to family, neighbors, and friends before they turn to any agency.⁹ Most search and rescue in the first 24 hours is performed by community members. People with trucks clear roads. Nurses triage on front porches. Teenagers who speak three languages become the only translators for miles.

Organizations

Deployed within hours

Small nonprofits with four-person staffs manage thousands of requests on spreadsheets. Large networks coordinate across hundreds of member organizations. Faith-based groups, mutual aid networks, and volunteer teams self-organize through group chats, Facebook posts, and word of mouth.

Government & Emergency Services

Overwhelmed & underfunded

85% of state emergency management agencies cite infrastructure limitations as the primary barrier to adopting AI coordination tools.¹⁰ Only 25% of directors say their staff have the skills to manage emergency conditions effectively. Working with tools from a different era.

Coordination as a Force Multiplier

When coordination fails,
no amount of resources is enough.

When it fails

Three organizations can help one family. Without coordination, two never hear about the need. The third shows up a week late. The family called nine hotlines. Nobody tracks what was spent. After Katrina, that absence of accountability cost billions in fraud.¹¹

When it works

The shelter organization, the food distribution team, and the medical transport volunteer all receive the same match at the same time. Each knows what the other is doing. Every dollar traced. Every contribution attributed. The system learns — and feeds the next disaster.

The technology to make this possible didn't exist two years ago. It does now.

Everyone Is a Helper

This is what we learned in the field.

The same person who needs shelter today volunteers their generator tomorrow. The displaced family with a truck is clearing roads by morning. The person who called for help last week is running supply deliveries this week.

There is no clean line between the people who need help and the people who give it. Needs and offers coexist on the same person, sometimes on the same day.

SOS doesn't categorize people as survivors or helpers. In the system, there are people — connected to what they need and what they can offer. The person asking isn't just asking. They're also contributing.

The pay-it-forward instinct is already there. What they lack isn't motivation.

It's infrastructure.

SOS is built on the premise that every person has something to offer — and that the coordination layer should see both sides. Requests and resources. Needs and offers. On every person. All the time.

The Solution

Relief as permaculture.

SOS is coordination infrastructure that connects citizens, nonprofits, government agencies, emergency services, and vendors — so that when someone needs help and someone else can provide it, the connection actually happens.

01

Intake — any device, any channel

Someone texts a number from a shelter. Someone scans a QR code on a flyer. Someone calls from a landline because their cell service is gone. They talk naturally — "my family needs a place to stay, we lost everything" — and the system extracts the need. No app to download. No account to create. Works on any phone.

02

Map — offers are captured the same way

People who want to help go through the same flow. "I have a truck and I'm in Asheville." "I can translate Spanish and Creole." "I have three pallets of water." The system captures what they can offer, where they are, and when they're available.

03

Match — simultaneous, intelligent coordination

The system finds the closest people who can respond. The match considers capability, capacity, proximity, and urgency. One family. Three people or organizations. Coordinated simultaneously. Each gets the match through their existing tools and accepts, declines, or reassigns.

04

Fulfill — every match traced

Every match is traced. Every outcome recorded. Each partner organization gets its own AI coordination agent — trained on their inventory, workflows, and rhythm. These agents retain context across every conversation, so each interaction builds on everything that came before.

05

Learn — the system compounds

After the first disaster season, the system knows which neighborhoods surge first, which partners respond within the hour, which resources run dry. Every cycle feeds the next. The intelligence compounds. The network strengthens. The response accelerates.

The system doesn't just respond to disasters — it learns from them. The disaster restoration market is $45 billion.¹² Revenue from connecting homeowners with vetted contractors funds coordination for people with no insurance, no savings, and no safety net. The mission is legally protected through a dual structure: a 501(c)(3) that is always free for citizens, and a Public Benefit Corporation that generates revenue and reinvests it into the mission.

THE ORIGIN

SOS started during Hurricane Helene. Our founder's farmhouse in Burnsville, North Carolina was damaged — he lived in a donated RV for three months. The team scraped 4,000 missing persons off Facebook, delivered RVs, and brought Starlink terminals to communities cut off for weeks. Since Helene, SOS has activated for every major disaster — including the Blizzard of 2026, coordinating hundreds of resources across 12 states.¹³

THE RESEARCH

After 18 months studying the history of disaster coordination and the science of coordination itself, SOS is production software. AI coordination agents are live with partner organizations across housing, food, medical, supplies, transport, and coordination. The citizen portal is live. The coordination loop is running.

THE VALUES

Partners keep their data. If they leave, it goes with them. Citizens control their privacy — no profile required, location data expires. Every coordination decision is logged with reasoning. SOS doesn't sell data, build profiles, or optimize for engagement. Communities deserve infrastructure that works for them, not on them. The values are structural, not aspirational.
