After Hurricane Helene hit western North Carolina in September 2024, FEMA's resource tracking system crashed under ten times its normal volume.³ Search and rescue teams checked the same homes three and four times because two platforms didn't talk to each other. Facebook became the missing persons database — not by design, but because nothing else worked.

This pattern isn't new. From Katrina to Sandy, Harvey to Maria, the same coordination failures surface every time. The tools break. The data fragments. The people who want to help can't find the people who need it.

Federal capacity is contracting

FEMA has stopped funding non-lifesaving disaster recovery. Twenty states have sued to restart the largest federal preparedness grant program.⁴ GAO added disaster assistance to its high-risk list for the first time.⁵

The gap is global

Humanitarian funding dropped 40 percent in a single year. The Council on Foreign Relations calls it "The Great Aid Recession." The disasters are accelerating. The systems are contracting.

Coordination as a Force Multiplier

Coordination as a force multiplier.

When coordination fails

A family needs shelter, food, and medical transport. Three organizations can help. Without coordination, two never hear about the need. The third shows up a week late. The family called nine hotlines and filled out fifteen applications — to organizations that have no way of knowing what the others have already done. Nobody tracks what was spent. After Katrina, that absence of accountability cost billions in fraud.⁹

When coordination works

The shelter organization, the food distribution team, and the medical transport volunteer all receive the same match at the same time. One family. Three partners. Connected simultaneously. Each knows what the other is doing. Every dollar is traced from source to outcome. Every contribution attributed. The fraud surface shrinks because the data trail is auditable. And the system learns.

When coordination fails — and today, it fails by default — the cost is measured in lives, in money lost to fraud, in communities that never fully recover, and in helpers who burn out because the system made them carry it alone.

The technology to make this possible didn't exist two years ago. It does now.

Relief as Permaculture

Relief as permaculture.

SOS is coordination infrastructure that connects citizens, nonprofits, government agencies, emergency services, and vendors — so that when someone needs help and someone else can provide it, the connection actually happens.

Step 1

Intake

Text, QR, call, web. No app. No account. Works on any phone. People talk naturally — "my family needs a place to stay" — and the system extracts the need.

Step 2

Match

The system finds the closest people who can respond — capability, capacity, proximity, urgency. One family. Three partners. Coordinated simultaneously.

Step 3

Coordinate

Each partner organization gets its own AI coordination agent — trained on their inventory, workflows, and rhythm. Context retained across every conversation.

Step 4

Fulfill

Every match is traced. Every dollar tracked from source to outcome. Every contribution attributed. The fraud surface shrinks because the data trail is auditable.

Step 5

Learn

The system knows which neighborhoods surge first, which partners respond within the hour, which resources run dry. That intelligence feeds the next disaster. The coordination compounds.

The system doesn't just respond to disasters — it learns from them. Every cycle feeds the next. The intelligence compounds. The network strengthens.

The system sustains itself. Relief as permaculture.

Revenue from connecting homeowners with vetted contractors funds coordination for people with no insurance, no savings, no safety net.
