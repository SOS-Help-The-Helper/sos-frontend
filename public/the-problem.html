The Problem

The disaster within the disaster.

The help always shows up. What fails is the system that’s supposed to coordinate it — and it fails the same way in every disaster.

What breaks

The tools break. The data fragments.

After Hurricane Helene hit western North Carolina in September 2024, FEMA’s resource tracking system crashed under ten times its normal volume. Search and rescue teams checked the same homes three and four times because two platforms didn’t talk to each other. The 211 system was overwhelmed. Families couldn’t find each other. Facebook became the missing-persons database — not by design, but because nothing else worked.

Volunteers drove supplies from across the country and were turned away at the edge of the damage zone — a phenomenon researchers call “volunteer convergence,” the secondary disaster within the disaster. Not because help wasn’t needed. Because nobody could tell them where to bring it.

This pattern isn’t new. From Katrina to Sandy, Harvey to Maria, the same coordination failures surface every time a disaster overwhelms the systems built to manage it.

NC DPS Hurricane Helene After-Action Review · ncdps.gov

What’s changing

The systems are contracting.

The federal safety net is not expanding to meet the moment — it’s contracting. FEMA has scaled back funding for non-lifesaving disaster recovery. Twenty states sued to restart the largest federal preparedness grant program. The GAO added disaster assistance to its high-risk list for the first time.

Humanitarian funding dropped 40 percent in a single year — what the Council on Foreign Relations calls “The Great Aid Recession.” The disasters are accelerating. The gap between people who need help and people who receive it is widening every year.

The cost of no coordination

One family. Nine hotlines. Fifteen applications.

A family needs shelter, food, and medical transport. Three organizations can help. Without coordination, two never hear about the need — and the third shows up a week late. The family called nine hotlines and filled out fifteen applications, to organizations that have no way of knowing what the others have already done.

No one tracks what was spent, who delivered what, or whether the help arrived. After Katrina, that absence of accountability cost over a billion dollars in documented fraud, with broader estimates reaching $6–8 billion. Without a coordination layer, every disaster repeats the same blind spots — and the system never learns from it.

GAO-06-844t (Katrina fraud) · gao.gov

Why it was never solved

The infrastructure never existed.

In 1986, Congress passed the Goldwater-Nichols Act to force the military branches to coordinate. It worked. Nobody ever passed the equivalent for civilian disaster response.

The organizations doing this work are extraordinary — they just can’t see each other. Search and rescue teams duplicate efforts while entire communities go unseen. Information blasts on social media go stale and turn to noise. The willingness is there. The money is there. The people are there.

They just can’t see each other.

The technology to close this gap didn’t exist two years ago. It does now.
