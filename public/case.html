The Case for SOS

In 2025, 305 million people needed humanitarian assistance. 207 million of them didn't receive it. Humanitarian funding dropped 40 percent in a single year. The Council on Foreign Relations coined a term for what's happening: "The Great Aid Recession."

This isn't a temporary dip. It's a structural collapse. The gap between people who need help and people who receive it has been widening for a decade—from 57 million in 2015 to 305 million today, a 430 percent increase. Meanwhile, the number of people actually reached is declining: 128 million in 2023, 116 million in 2024, 98 million in 2025. More people need help every year. Fewer people get it. The funding shortfall hit $24.4 billion.

In the United States, the picture is equally grim. FEMA has stopped funding non-lifesaving disaster recovery. Reimbursements to states are halting. Twenty states have sued to restart the largest federal preparedness grant program. The GAO added disaster assistance to its high-risk list for the first time in the program's history. The federal safety net isn't expanding to meet the moment. It's contracting.

You might assume this means disaster response is getting worse. It's not. The response to every major disaster is enormous—bigger, faster, and more capable than any system gives it credit for. The problem is that the response is invisible to the systems that are supposed to coordinate it.

There's a pattern in American disaster response that's been repeating for twenty years. It goes like this: a catastrophic event overwhelms the existing infrastructure. Communications collapse. Agencies can't talk to each other. Volunteers flood in but nobody can tell them where to go. Resources pile up in the wrong places. The same homes get checked three times while others never get checked at all. And then, months later, someone writes an after-action report documenting exactly what went wrong, and everyone agrees it shouldn't happen again.

Then it happens again.

Hurricane Katrina hit the Gulf Coast in 2005. FEMA's director promised the governor of Louisiana 500 buses to evacuate the Superdome. The buses never came Monday. They never came Tuesday. They never came Wednesday. FEMA didn't actually request buses from the Department of Transportation until 1:45 AM on Wednesday. The buses arrived Thursday. Thirty thousand people were stranded in 100-degree heat without food, water, or basic sanitation for days—not because buses didn't exist, but because no one tracked the promise through delivery.

The military sent search and rescue teams to the same neighborhoods while others went uncovered. Federal managers couldn't determine what resources were available, where they were, or what was needed. Three million customers lost phone service. Active duty forces and National Guard operated under separate command structures with zero visibility of each other's positions. It wasn't a failure of effort. It was a failure of coordination.

When Lt. General Russel Honoré arrived at the Convention Center to take command, he found soldiers and police pointing weapons at survivors. A mother named Alexandra Wheeler was holding her dehydrated five-month-old twins in terror.

"Put those damn weapons down. They're the victims. You're not in combat."

He took one of her twins and walked the family to a Coast Guard vessel for medical treatment. The twins, now in their twenties, both went on to military careers.

Honoré understood something fundamental. His most structurally important observation was about why coordination kept failing. Congress had passed the Goldwater-Nichols Act to force the military branches to work together. Before Goldwater-Nichols, the Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marines fought their own wars, in their own lanes, with their own budgets. Congress said: enough. You will be joint. You will share information. You will coordinate.

"You gave us Goldwater-Nichols, and it was a bitter pill to swallow. You've got a joint, dependent, interagency, knock-'em-down Department of Defense. You don't have that in the interagency. Our friends in the interagency don't approach the joint interdependence the same way you forced us down that road."

Nobody ever passed a Goldwater-Nichols for civilian disaster response.

Twelve years later, Hurricane Harvey hit Houston. Record rainfall displaced 780,000 people. Harris County received 26,000 calls for service in seven days. Multiple rescue agencies were dispatched to the same calls because there was no shared dispatch system. A thousand civilian responders self-deployed without coordination. Fifty-four independent fire departments, seven standalone EMS agencies, and over a hundred law enforcement agencies tried to work together in a single county with no common operating picture.

The same year, Hurricane Maria hit Puerto Rico. Ninety-five percent of cell towers went down. A hundred percent of the power grid went offline. Handwritten resource requests had to be reviewed manually because the digital systems failed. Shipping containers arrived labeled simply "disaster supplies"—they had to be unloaded to determine what was inside.

We compiled the after-action reports from Katrina, Harvey, Maria, and every major hurricane in between. We tracked fourteen core structural problems across two decades: no real-time resource tracking, competing command structures, plans that assumed single incidents, duplicate rescue efforts, communications failures, no electronic health records, housing delays, no single point of contact for victims, vulnerable populations hit hardest, behavioral health underserved, exercise lessons not incorporated, insufficient pre-disaster relationships.

The number of those fourteen problems that were solved between 2005 and 2017 is zero. Every single one repeated. Most got worse.

Then came Helene.

In September 2024, Hurricane Helene hit western North Carolina. FEMA's resource tracking system crashed under roughly ten times its normal volume. Two search-and-rescue platforms operated with zero integration. Facebook became the missing persons database. Volunteers drove supplies from across the country and were turned away at the edge of the damage zone—the secondary disaster within the disaster.

Our founder's farmhouse in Burnsville, North Carolina was damaged. He lived in a donated RV for three months. When roads washed out and isolated mountain communities, neighbors carved access trails through the terrain themselves. The team scraped over 4,000 missing persons posts from Facebook because no system existed to track them. They delivered RVs, brought Starlink terminals to people who'd been cut off for weeks, and started building the coordination tools that should have existed before the storm hit.

But here's what the after-action reports always miss: while the formal systems failed, the actual response was extraordinary. Research shows that after a major emergency, three out of four people turn to family, neighbors, and friends before any agency. Most search and rescue in the first 24 hours is performed by community members, not professional responders. People with trucks clear roads. Nurses triage on front porches. Teenagers who speak three languages become the only translators for miles. An elderly couple in their eighties—their own home damaged—gets up before dawn to care for the properties of people who had to evacuate.

The help is there. It has always been there. What's missing is the ability for all of these people to see each other.

If you want to understand why this problem hasn't been solved, you have to understand the trust deficit.

In Appalachia—the region hit hardest by Helene—communities have watched extraction for generations. Coal companies stripped the mountains, took the wealth, and left environmental devastation. Now rare earth minerals in coal refuse are a federal priority, FEMA buyouts offer below-market prices to flood-damaged families, data centers expand into cheap-power Appalachian land, and tourism trails are built through cleared former mining areas.

Nobody is orchestrating this. Multiple systems—FEMA, the Department of Energy, the Department of Transportation, private capital—are independently optimizing for their own incentives on the same geography. But the cumulative effect on communities is the same whether it's coordinated or not. People who've watched every wave of extraction see another platform showing up to "help" and they have zero reason to trust it.

This trust deficit is the hardest problem in disaster response. It's harder than the technology. It's harder than the logistics. It's also why the volunteer system is broken in ways that go beyond disasters. Fifty-four percent of nonprofits can't measure or communicate the impact of their volunteers. Forty-three percent of volunteers quit because they didn't feel they belonged. Forty-four percent of potential volunteers never even started because they weren't sure they'd belong.

The pattern is the same everywhere. People want to help. The infrastructure to connect them doesn't exist. And the systems that do exist are designed to manage people from the top down, not to help people coordinate with each other.

Here is what we learned in the field, and it changed everything about how we designed SOS.

There is no clean line between the people who need help and the people who give it. The same person who needs shelter today volunteers their generator tomorrow. The displaced family with a truck is clearing roads by morning. The nurse triaging injuries on her porch needs someone to watch her kids. The volunteer driving an RV across two states also had their own roof damaged.

Everyone is a helper.

This isn't a slogan. It's a design principle. The traditional model categorizes people as either survivors or responders. That binary is wrong. Needs and offers coexist on the same person, sometimes on the same day. Any coordination system that doesn't see both sides—what they need and what they can offer—is leaving most of the available capacity invisible.

SOS is coordination infrastructure. It connects citizens, nonprofits, government agencies, emergency services, and vendors so that when someone needs help and someone else can provide it, the connection actually happens.

Someone texts a number from a shelter. Someone else scans a QR code on a flyer. Someone calls from a landline because their cell service is gone. They talk naturally—"my family needs a place to stay, we lost everything"—and the system extracts the need. No app to download. No account to create. Works on any phone, in any language.

People who want to help go through the same flow. "I have a truck and I'm in Asheville." "I can translate Spanish and Creole." "I have three pallets of water." The system captures what they can offer, where they are, and when they're available.

The system finds the closest people who can respond—a neighbor with a spare room, a nonprofit with available housing, a volunteer driver, an agency with open case management. One family. Three helpers. Connected simultaneously. Each gets the match through their existing tools and accepts, declines, or reassigns.

Every match is traced. Every outcome recorded. Every coordination event makes the next one smarter.

The obvious question is: doesn't something like this already exist?

The short answer is no.

RapidSOS has raised over $450 million to make 911 calls smarter. They connect 700 million devices to 22,000 public safety agencies. But they optimize the emergency call—the data that flows from your phone to a dispatcher. When 911 is overwhelmed or down, which is exactly what happens in a catastrophic disaster, RapidSOS has nothing to offer. Zero community-facing capability. No volunteer coordination. No disaster recovery.

Crisis Cognition builds offline AI hardware for disaster zones—a Raspberry Pi with a solar panel running project management software for field teams. It solves a real problem for organizations deployed in austere environments. But it's a tool for responders, not a coordination layer for communities.

Everyone in this space is solving pieces of the institutional side. Better dispatch. Better field tools. Better data for agencies. Nobody is solving the community side—the vast majority of disaster response that happens outside formal systems, between people who want to help and people who need it.

The UN's Logistics Cluster runs the most sophisticated common operating picture in the world. They map road passability, storage capacity, and supply chains across conflict zones in Sudan and Ethiopia. Even they can't close the last-mile gap. They can track what's in their warehouses. They can't connect the cargo at the end of the road to the person who needs it. In one Ethiopian region, 851 out of 1,553 communities are inaccessible—226,000 people cut off from essential services. The gap between "resources exist" and "resources reach people" is the defining challenge.

That gap is what SOS is built to close.

The way SOS pays for itself matters as much as how it works.

The disaster restoration market—contractors, equipment suppliers, insurance adjusters—is $45 billion annually. After Katrina, the GAO documented over a billion dollars in fraudulent disaster payments, with broader estimates reaching $6 to $8 billion. Every major disaster produces a wave of price gouging, unlicensed contractors, and recovery fraud.

SOS builds trust scoring into the vendor layer. Verified outcomes. Reviews that compound. Accountability that follows. The revenue generated by connecting homeowners with vetted contractors flows back into funding coordination for people who have no insurance, no savings, and no safety net.

The mission is legally protected through a dual structure: a 501(c)(3) that is always free for citizens and the organizations that serve them, and a Public Benefit Corporation that generates revenue and reinvests it. The free coordination layer for communities is funded by the trust layer for the disaster economy. Not grant-dependent. Not venture-dependent. Sustained by the economic activity that disasters generate anyway.

The system doesn't just respond to disasters—it learns from them. Every cycle feeds the next. After the first disaster season, the system knows which neighborhoods surge first, which partners respond within the hour, which resources run dry, which skills resolve needs faster. That accumulated understanding of how communities coordinate in crisis is the most valuable dataset in disaster response—not because it was extracted, but because it was earned through real coordination.

Disaster response is where SOS starts. It's not where it ends.

The same infrastructure that connects a survivor to a neighbor with a spare room could connect any citizen's skills to a community need. The same trust layer that holds disaster contractors accountable could hold institutions accountable year-round. The same feedback loop that surfaces post-disaster needs could give communities a real-time voice in how resources are allocated.

Twenty years of after-action reports say the same thing: the problems are known, the solutions are obvious, and nobody builds the infrastructure. The military had to be forced by legislation to coordinate. Civilian agencies never were. And while the formal systems argue about jurisdiction and process, communities keep doing what they've always done—showing up for each other with whatever they have.

They just need a way to find each other.

Everyone is a helper. We help the helpers.
