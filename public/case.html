Team Brief

Everyone Is a Helper

A brief to the team — where we are, where we’re going, and why it matters.

Jonathan Greenberg · Founder, SOS · April 2026
↓

I want to tell you about Stanley and Judy. They’re in their eighties. They live up a mountain road outside Burnsville, North Carolina — the kind of place where your neighbor mows your fields when you’re away and your neighbor’s wife becomes a substitute mother. When Helene hit in September 2024, the road to their farm washed out completely. Six days. No cell service, no power, no way to tell their daughter they were alive.

On the seventh morning, their neighbors started building a road. Not a government crew. Neighbors. They found gravel from a nearby job site and an excavator from somewhere nobody’s entirely sure about, and they carved an ATV trail through the wreckage. My buddy Dan in Charlotte has a couple of side-by-sides — I called him, we loaded up a Starlink, drove it up there. When Stanley and Judy got internet again and could call their family for the first time in a week, the look on their faces — that communicates everything about what we’re trying to do.

I went through Helene myself. My farmhouse in Burnsville took bad damage. Insurance didn’t cover the plumbing — rodent infestation clause, the kind of thing you don’t think about until you’re standing in your kitchen watching a plumber shake his head. Emergency RV got us a trailer to live in for three months. While that was happening, I scraped four thousand missing persons posts off Facebook because 211 was down. I did RV deliveries — drove one from Florida to North Carolina with my brothers from Boston. And I started building what should have existed before the storm hit.

• • •

Here’s what I learned.

Eighty-five percent of people who get help after a disaster — the first point of contact is citizens. Not emergency management services. Not FEMA. Neighbors. A PhD student and a thrift store owner in Cocke County, Tennessee pulling every address in the county and knocking on doors because nobody else was going to check. Paul and Amy Dubuque driving their personal camper across Virginia to hand the keys to a young couple named Dante and Raven who’d lost everything. “They seem like a wonderful young couple,” Amy wrote. That’s the real texture of disaster response — not situation rooms, but a retired couple handing their keys to strangers.

And the organizations. Emergency RV started with one person after the Camp Fire in Paradise, California in 2018 — now they’ve delivered over 160 RVs to survivor families, working with the state of Florida to coordinate five hundred donations. On seven spreadsheets. Free Hot Meals feeds thousands. The VOADs coordinate dozens of member nonprofits in every region. Red Cross deploys within hours. These are extraordinary people doing extraordinary work.

The coordinator at SBCVOAD in San Bernardino — a guy named Eric who’s been through floods himself — told us what practitioners actually need. “Ideally it wouldn’t require them to download an app. You’re able to get basic information via text.” That’s the bar. Not a platform. Not a dashboard. Something so simple it works when everything else is broken.

There’s this feeling inside people to help others. SOS is about making it easier for them to find each other.
• • •

But when they can’t — when that coordination fails — the cost is not abstract.

A family that gets temporary housing within seventy-two hours has dramatically better recovery outcomes than one waiting thirty days. Every day of delay compounds. Delayed housing becomes job loss. Job loss becomes the need for unemployment benefits, food assistance, Medicaid. Mental health doesn’t gradually decline — it predictably collapses. After Katrina, PTSD prevalence among survivors increased over time. Fifteen percent at five months. Twenty-one percent at a year. A third still struggling years later. After Sandy, researchers found something cruel: the people with the highest PTSD rates weren’t the ones whose homes were destroyed. They were the people with minor, unrepaired damage — people who could almost see normal but couldn’t reach it.

Three million Americans are displaced by disasters every year. A quarter to nearly half of them for longer than a month. Survivors calling Red Cross, FEMA, Emergency RV — nineteen different providers — filling out the same twenty fields at ten different booths. The system doesn’t just fail to help. When it fails, it actively makes things worse.

And the formal system is contracting. FEMA has stopped funding non-lifesaving disaster recovery. Twelve senior leaders gone. Six hundred million in grants clawed back. The GAO added disaster assistance to its high-risk list for the first time. Globally, the Council on Foreign Relations calls it “The Great Aid Recession” — 305 million people needed help in 2025, 207 million didn’t get it. The safety net is shrinking at the exact moment the disasters are accelerating.

• • •

This is a coordination problem. And it’s not new — it’s twenty years old and it has never been solved.

In 2005, FEMA’s director promised the governor of Louisiana five hundred buses to evacuate the Superdome. They didn’t arrive until Thursday. Thirty thousand people, four days, hundred-degree heat — not because buses didn’t exist, but because nobody tracked a promise through delivery. When Lt. General Russel Honoré arrived at the Convention Center and found soldiers pointing weapons at survivors, he took a mother’s dehydrated baby in his arms and walked the family to a Coast Guard vessel. Then he told the Senate something that should have changed everything.

Congress had forced the military to coordinate with Goldwater-Nichols. It was a bitter pill. But it worked. “You don’t have that in the interagency,” Honoré said. Nobody ever forced civilian disaster agencies to work together. That was 2006. It’s still true.

Nobody ever passed a Goldwater-Nichols for civilian disaster response.

MIT researchers built the science of coordination in the nineties — Malone and Crowston showed that when coordination fails, it’s not because people didn’t try, it’s because no mechanism existed to manage the dependency. The World Bank reached the same conclusion: coordination failure is an infrastructure problem, not a motivation problem. And when the infrastructure exists, the math changes — network effects mean every new participant exponentially increases the system’s capacity. Stanley’s twenty neighbors would have been ten times more effective if they could see what every other group of neighbors in Yancey County was doing at the same time.

We tracked fourteen structural problems across every after-action report from Katrina through Helene. The number solved between 2005 and 2024 is zero. Not because the organizations failed. Because the infrastructure to connect them never existed.

• • •

I’ve been telling people SOS is the anti-Palantir. Because transparency and trust — especially with people’s personal information — that’s foundational for us. The brand we’re building has to be about trust. AI agents, but humans in the loop. People enabled and empowered to use these tools so they can focus on what they’re actually good at.

The thesis is simple: everyone is a helper.

People are at different levels and stages of either needing help or giving help. Someone who just lost their home — they’re a survivor. But I’ve seen those same people helping others at the same time. We capture that in the code, literally in the database. People are tied to requests and resources, to partner organizations, to vendors who can contribute. You’re never just one thing. You’re a person who hit a certain point in your life, and what you need and what you can offer are both real, both valid, at the same time.

Everyone is a helper.

That’s not a tagline. It’s a field observation — and it turns out to be a neurological fact.

The pay-it-forward movement showed that you get a real psychological benefit from helping without expecting anything in return — and it creates a wave of others who want to feel it too. Neuroscience confirms it: helping others triggers oxytocin, dopamine, and serotonin. Researchers call it the helper’s high. A 2025 study in the Journal of Neuroscience found that higher oxytocin receptor levels directly drive helping behavior — it’s built into our neural architecture.

Helping isn’t learned behavior. It’s how we’re wired. And it cascades.

Research in Nature’s Scientific Reports found that one act of generosity inspires an average of three more along the chain. The instinct isn’t just there. It multiplies. And any coordination system that only sees one side of a person is leaving most of the available capacity on earth invisible.

• • •

We’re not the first people to try to solve this. But AI brings a new layer. And the technology wasn’t ready before — I want to be clear about that.

Two years ago, conversational AI couldn’t reliably extract structured information from someone texting “my roof collapsed, three kids, no car, no power.” Agentic AI — systems that take actions, coordinate across parties, hold context across hundreds of conversations — is a 2025 and 2026 development. It didn’t exist in usable form when Honoré was testifying. And then in April 2026, Google released Gemma 4, open-source, on-device — AI that runs on a phone with no internet, no cell signal, a hundred and forty languages. When ninety-five percent of cell towers are down, that changes everything.

SOS is community coordination infrastructure. Conversational agents replace government forms with two-minute natural language intake — SMS, WhatsApp, voice, web. No app, no account, any language, any phone. A matching engine inspired by the organ transplant network scores connections across proximity, capability, urgency. Every match traced, every outcome recorded.

Each nonprofit gets its own agent trained for its tasks, and they all roll up to the main SOS coordination layer. Emergency RV’s agent knows about three-way matches between survivors, RVs, and drivers. Free Hot Meals’ agent knows that food distribution is time-sensitive. The institutional knowledge that used to live in one person’s brain now persists across every event.

The revenue model: the disaster restoration market is forty-one billion dollars, rampant with fraud — over three billion in improper FEMA payments, a hundred and five thousand dollars per FEMA trailer versus five thousand for a donated RV through ERV. We build trust scoring into the vendor layer. Revenue from connecting homeowners with vetted contractors funds coordination for people who have nothing. A 501(c)(3) that’s always free for citizens and the orgs that serve them. A Public Benefit Corporation that generates revenue and reinvests it.

We started onboarding nonprofits this spring. Five active partners. Real matching — survivor to resource to volunteer. During the Blizzard of 2026, we compiled 292 resources across nine states. Fox Weather covered it. Luke, an Army veteran who did three weeks of SAR during Helene, produced eighteen product ideas for us and is presenting SOS at the FEMA and DHS Irregular Warfare symposium in August.

Twelve agents in production today. The roadmap: a hundred partner agents, then a thousand community agents — each trained on a specific neighborhood’s patterns, organizations, and volunteers. Then ten thousand communities. What accumulates across all of those events is something that’s never existed: a coordination science dataset. Not a database of needs — those exist. A dataset of decisions. Which matches worked and why. Which communities recovered fastest. Which resources run dry first. Evidence that could shape policy and funding for decades. Not because the data was extracted, but because it was earned by the people closest to the problem.

• • •

I always felt, volunteering at different projects over the years — what is mine? People at HubSpot would say, I really want to work at the women’s shelter, or I have a passion for food insecurity. I never had one until life brought this to me. And that’s when things clicked.

My dream for SOS is this:

Communities need tools to surface information, match people with that information, and give them the ability to make changes at the community level.

Not just every four years when there’s a vote. Continuously. Disaster relief is where we start. But the same coordination infrastructure handles mutual aid year-round. Civic engagement. Community resource sharing. The same trust layer that holds contractors accountable after a hurricane holds them accountable when they’re repaving your street.

I think in five or ten years, there’s going to be coordination science — real data — that helps communities better self-govern, reposition resources, and understand who’s actually helping versus who’s extracting. We’re building that foundation now.

Victoria and I have spent eighteen months doing the research, talking to the people on the ground, building the technology, and learning from every disaster that hit while we were building. We’re not done. We’re getting started. The more realistic path isn’t top-down — it’s nonprofits who need something better than spreadsheets, connected with citizens who want to help, building a groundswell of people who find real value from coordination that works.

This is the team brief. This is where we are. This is where we’re going.

Everyone is a helper. We’re building the infrastructure to prove it. And everyone who shows up is part of it.
