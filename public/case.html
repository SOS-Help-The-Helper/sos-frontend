The Case for SOS

The humanitarian system is contracting. The disasters are accelerating. And the biggest coordination network on earth has no infrastructure.

SOS Research Team · April 2026 · 11 min read

When Lt. General Russel Honoré arrived at the New Orleans Convention Center on the fourth day after Hurricane Katrina, he found thirty thousand people stranded in hundred-degree heat, and he found soldiers and police pointing weapons at them. A mother named Alexandra Wheeler was holding her dehydrated five-month-old twins in terror. Honoré—born during a hurricane himself, raised on a subsistence farm in Lakeland, Louisiana, one of twelve children—barked a command that would define the entire federal response:

“Put those damn weapons down. They’re the victims. You’re not in combat.”

Then he did something no general is supposed to do. He took one of Wheeler’s twins in his arms and walked the family to a Coast Guard vessel for medical treatment. The twins are in their twenties now. Both went on to military careers.

Nobody remembers this part of Katrina. What they remember is the failure. FEMA’s director promised the governor of Louisiana 500 buses to evacuate the Superdome. The buses never came Monday. They never came Tuesday. They never came Wednesday. FEMA didn’t actually request buses from the Department of Transportation until 1:45 AM on Wednesday. The buses arrived Thursday. Four days. Thirty thousand people. Not because buses didn’t exist, but because no one tracked the promise through delivery.

It was not a failure of effort. It was not a failure of will. It was a failure of coordination.

Honoré understood this better than anyone in the room. Congress, he reminded the Senate, had passed the Goldwater-Nichols Act in 1986 to force the military branches to work together. Before Goldwater-Nichols, the Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marines fought their own wars, in their own lanes, with their own budgets. The result was catastrophic redundancy and catastrophic gaps—the same territory covered three times, the territory next door not covered at all.

“You gave us Goldwater-Nichols, and it was a bitter pill to swallow. You’ve got a joint, dependent, interagency, knock-’em-down Department of Defense. You don’t have that in the interagency.”

Nobody ever passed a Goldwater-Nichols for civilian disaster response.
• • •

If that failure were a one-time event, you could write it off as a learning moment. But the pattern has repeated, almost identically, for twenty years.

Hurricane Harvey, 2017. Record rainfall displaced 780,000 people in Houston. Fifty-four independent fire departments, seven standalone EMS agencies, and over a hundred law enforcement agencies tried to work together in a single county with no common operating picture. Hurricane Maria, the same year. Ninety-five percent of Puerto Rico’s cell towers went down. The entire power grid went offline. FEMA had 695 generators for 2,273 requests.

We tracked fourteen core structural problems across the after-action reports from Katrina through Maria. The number solved between 2005 and 2017 is zero. Every single one repeated. Most got worse.

Then came Helene.

In September 2024, Hurricane Helene hit western North Carolina—the mountains, not the coast, which is part of why nobody saw it coming the way they should have. FEMA’s resource tracking system, WebEOC, crashed under roughly ten times its normal volume. Two search-and-rescue platforms operated with zero integration, so teams checked the same homes three and four times while others were never checked at all. Facebook became the missing persons database. Not by design. Because nothing else worked.

And then came the volunteers. Thousands of them, driving trucks loaded with supplies into the mountains. Researchers call what happened next “volunteer convergence”—the secondary disaster within the disaster. Not because help wasn’t needed. Because nobody could tell them where to bring it. Drop sites overflowed. The same pattern had played out weeks earlier during the Nebraska and Oklahoma wildfires, where hay convoys organized via Facebook DMs stretched across fourteen states—seven-hour drives to staging areas that were already full.

The help was massive. The help was real. And the help had nowhere to go.
• • •

Here’s what the after-action reports never capture.

Jonathan Greenberg’s farmhouse in Burnsville, North Carolina was damaged by Helene. He lived in a donated RV for three months. His neighbors didn’t wait for FEMA—they carved access trails through the terrain with ATVs. His team scraped over 4,000 missing persons posts from Facebook because no system existed to track them. They delivered RVs. They brought Starlink terminals to people who’d been cut off from the outside world for weeks. And they started building the coordination tools that should have existed before the storm hit.

But Greenberg’s experience wasn’t unusual. It was the norm.

Eighty-five percent of survivors get initial help from neighbors, not agencies. Most search and rescue in the first twenty-four hours is performed by community members. People with trucks clear roads. Nurses triage injuries on front porches. Teenagers who speak three languages become the only translators for miles.

In Bald Creek, North Carolina, a grassroots food distribution network emerged where volunteers photographed incoming donations and used AI to calculate servings—not because someone designed a system, but because the people on the ground needed to know how much food they actually had. In another part of the disaster zone, a University of Tennessee geography PhD student and a thrift store owner created Operation Love Your Neighbor—a systematic door-to-door welfare check system built on county address data. They knocked on every door. They used what they had: a database, a car, and the conviction that someone should be checking.

Paul and Amy Dubuque donated their personal camper to a young couple named Dante and Raven through Emergency RV, one of the grassroots organizations matching displaced families with donated recreational vehicles since the Camp Fire in Paradise, California in 2018. “I think they both were a little bit overwhelmed to receive the camper,” Amy said afterward. That’s the texture of real disaster response—not press conferences and situation rooms, but a retired couple handing the keys to their camper to strangers because it was the right thing to do.

The help is there. It has always been there. What’s missing is the ability for all of these people to see each other.

According to OCHA, 305 million people needed humanitarian assistance in 2025. 207 million didn’t receive it. Humanitarian funding dropped 40 percent in the first half of 2025—the Council on Foreign Relations calls it “The Great Aid Recession.” In the United States, the GAO added disaster assistance to its high-risk list for the first time. FEMA is being restructured: twelve senior leaders departed, $600 million in grants clawed back, North Carolina residents still waiting over a year for aid. The federal safety net is not expanding to meet the moment. It is contracting.

And the response from the ground? It’s growing. Every disaster produces more volunteers, more mutual aid networks, more grassroots organizations than the one before. The gap between what people are willing to do and what the infrastructure allows them to do is the defining crisis of modern disaster response.

• • •

If you want to understand why this problem hasn’t been solved, you have to understand the trust deficit.

In Appalachia—the region hit hardest by Helene—communities have watched extraction for generations. Coal companies stripped the mountains and left devastation. Now FEMA buyouts offer below-market prices to flood-damaged families, data centers are expanding into cheap-power Appalachian land, and multiple federal systems are independently optimizing for their own incentives on the same geography. People who’ve watched every wave of extraction see another platform from the outside showing up to “help” and they have zero reason to trust it.

You can see this playing out at disaster recovery centers right now. Survivors sit down at table after table, filling out the same twenty fields at ten different organizational booths—over and over, to organizations that have no way of knowing what the others have already collected. The technology to prevent this exists. The trust to deploy it hasn’t been built yet.

This is what Honoré understood at the Convention Center. Before you can coordinate, you have to stop treating people like threats. The posture of most disaster technology is extraction: collect data upward, aggregate it centrally, use it for institutional purposes. Communities can feel the difference between infrastructure that works for them and infrastructure that works on them.

There is a field insight that changed everything about how SOS was designed, and it came from months of living in the disaster zone, working alongside the people affected by it.

There is no clean line between the people who need help and the people who give it. The same person who needs shelter today volunteers their generator tomorrow. The displaced family with a truck is clearing roads by morning. A nurse triaging injuries on her porch needs someone to watch her kids. Paul and Amy Dubuque donated their camper—and they’d been through their own losses.

Everyone is a helper.

That’s not a tagline. It’s an observation about how human beings actually behave in crisis—one that overturns the fundamental assumption of every disaster response system ever built. Needs and offers coexist on the same person, sometimes on the same day. Any coordination system that doesn’t see both sides of every person is leaving most of the available capacity invisible.

• • •

SOS is community coordination infrastructure powered by agentic AI. It manages the full coordination lifecycle: intake, intelligent matching, logistics, fulfillment, and institutional learning—through decision traces that compound across every event.

Someone texts a number from a shelter. Someone else scans a QR code on a flyer. Someone calls from a landline because their cell service is gone. Conversational AI agents replace multi-page government forms with two-minute natural language intake across SMS, WhatsApp, web, and voice. No app to download. No account to create. Works on any phone, in any language. People who want to help go through the same flow—“I have a truck and I’m in Asheville,” “I can translate Spanish and Creole,” “I have three pallets of water.” The system captures what they can offer, where they are, when they’re available.

A matching engine inspired by UNOS—the organ transplant network—scores connections across proximity, capability, urgency, and vulnerability. One family. Three helpers. Connected simultaneously. Each gets the match through their existing tools. Every match is traced. Every outcome recorded. Every coordination event makes the next one smarter.

Each partner organization gets its own AI coordination agent, trained on their specific inventory, workflows, and rhythm. These agents retain context across every conversation, building institutional knowledge that previously lived only in one overworked coordinator’s head—the kind of knowledge that, in Bald Creek, had to be reinvented from scratch by volunteers counting servings from photographs.

The way SOS pays for itself matters as much as how it works. The $41.2 billion disaster restoration market is rampant with price gouging and recovery fraud—the GAO flagged disaster assistance as high-risk in 2025, and over $3 billion in FEMA payments went to unverified applicants. SOS builds trust scoring into the vendor layer. Revenue from connecting homeowners with vetted contractors funds coordination for people who have no insurance, no savings, and no safety net. The mission is protected through a dual structure: a 501(c)(3) that is always free for citizens and the organizations that serve them, and a Public Benefit Corporation that generates revenue and reinvests it.

The system doesn’t just respond to disasters. It learns from them. After each event, it knows which neighborhoods surge first, which partners respond within the hour, which resources run dry. That accumulated intelligence—earned through real coordination, not extracted—becomes the most valuable dataset in disaster response. Partners keep their data. Citizens control their privacy. No profiles. No surveillance. Communities deserve infrastructure that works for them, not on them.

Since Helene, SOS has activated for every major disaster. During the Blizzard of 2026, the platform compiled 292 geocoded resources across nine states with automated weather monitoring—Fox Weather covered the deployment nationally. Five partner organizations are doing real three-way matching—survivor to resource to volunteer—across housing, food, and multi-org coordination. The coordination loop is running. AI agents are in production.

• • •

Twenty years of after-action reports say the same thing: the problems are known, the solutions are obvious, and nobody builds the infrastructure. The military had to be forced by legislation to coordinate. Civilian agencies never were. Honoré saw this in 2005 and said it plainly to the United States Senate. Every disaster since has proved him right.

But here’s what’s different now. The technology to match needs with offers in real time—across languages, across channels, across organizational boundaries—didn’t exist when Honoré was carrying Alexandra Wheeler’s baby to a Coast Guard vessel. It didn’t exist when hay convoys were organized through Facebook DMs across fourteen states. It didn’t exist when Paul and Amy Dubuque were figuring out how to get their camper to a couple they’d never met.

It exists now.

The formal systems are contracting. The funding is drying up. And every disaster, without fail, produces the same extraordinary response from ordinary people who show up with whatever they have. They’ve been doing this forever. They’ll keep doing it whether or not anyone builds them infrastructure.

But the gap between what they’re willing to do and what they’re able to coordinate is the gap where people fall through. It’s where the same house gets checked four times and the house next door never gets checked at all. It’s where hay convoys drive seven hours to overflowing drop sites. It’s where survivors fill out the same twenty fields at ten different booths.

That gap has been documented in every after-action report for two decades. Honoré named it. The GAO flagged it. The field proved it.

SOS closes the gap. Not by replacing the people who show up—they don’t need replacing—but by giving them the one thing they’ve never had: a way to find each other.

Everyone is a helper.
The helpers just need infrastructure.
