Field Report

Everyone Is a Helper

Citizens are the actual first responders. They always have been. The infrastructure to connect them has never existed.

SOS Research Team · April 2026 · 18 min read
↓

Stanley is in his eighties. He lives up a mountain road outside Burnsville, North Carolina with his wife Judy. When Hurricane Helene hit in September 2024, the road to their farm was destroyed—not damaged, destroyed. Washed out completely. For six days, nobody could reach them. No cell service. No power. No way to tell their daughter they were alive.

On the seventh morning, their neighbors started building a road. Not a government crew, not FEMA, not the National Guard. Neighbors. They found gravel from a nearby job site and an excavator from somewhere—nobody’s entirely sure where—and they carved an ATV trail through the wreckage so that people could get in with supplies, with Starlink terminals so Stanley and Judy could call their family again, with the basic proof that someone was coming.

Eighty-five percent of people who receive help after a disaster get it first from citizens.

Not agencies. Not nonprofits. Neighbors. This is the part of disaster response that doesn’t make the news. The retired teacher who drives his truck six hours with donated supplies. The PhD student and the thrift store owner in Cocke County, Tennessee who pulled every residential address in the county and knocked on doors, one by one, to check on people—because nobody else was going to. Paul and Amy Dubuque, who drove their personal camper across Virginia to hand the keys to a young couple named Dante and Raven who’d lost everything. “I think they both were a little bit overwhelmed to receive the camper,” Amy wrote afterward. “They seem like a wonderful young couple.”

These are not edge cases. This is the response. Every single disaster, everywhere, this is what actually happens first.

• • •

Here’s what also happens.

When coordination fails—and today, it fails by default—the cost isn’t abstract. A family that gets temporary housing within seventy-two hours has dramatically better recovery outcomes than one waiting thirty days. Every day of delay compounds. Delayed housing leads to job loss. Job loss leads to the need for unemployment benefits, food assistance, Medicaid. Mental health declines—not gradually, but predictably. After Katrina, PTSD prevalence among survivors didn’t decrease over time. It increased. Fifteen percent at five months. Twenty-one percent at one year. Nearly a third of Katrina survivors were still struggling years later. After Sandy, researchers found that the people with the highest PTSD rates weren’t the ones whose homes were destroyed. They were the ones with minor, unrepaired damage—people who could almost see normal but couldn’t reach it.

Three million Americans are displaced by disasters every year. Between a quarter and nearly half of them are displaced for longer than a month. Many never fully return.

And the response system built to help them? It’s contracting. FEMA has stopped funding non-lifesaving disaster recovery. Twelve senior leaders have departed. Six hundred million dollars in grants clawed back. The GAO added disaster assistance to its high-risk list for the first time in history. North Carolina residents are still waiting for aid more than a year after Helene. The Council on Foreign Relations calls what’s happening globally “The Great Aid Recession”—305 million people needed humanitarian assistance in 2025, and 207 million of them didn’t receive it.

The formal safety net is shrinking. The disasters are accelerating. And every time, the same extraordinary grassroots response shows up—bigger, faster, and more capable than any system gives it credit for.

It just has no way to coordinate.

• • •

This isn’t a new problem. It’s a twenty-year-old problem that has never been solved.

In 2005, FEMA’s director promised the governor of Louisiana five hundred buses to evacuate the Superdome after Katrina. The buses never came Monday, Tuesday, or Wednesday. FEMA didn’t actually request them from the Department of Transportation until 1:45 AM on Wednesday. They arrived Thursday. Thirty thousand people waited in hundred-degree heat without food or water—not because buses didn’t exist, but because nobody tracked a promise through delivery.

When Lt. General Russel Honoré arrived at the New Orleans Convention Center, he found soldiers pointing weapons at survivors. A mother named Alexandra Wheeler was holding her dehydrated five-month-old twins. Honoré—born during a hurricane himself, one of twelve children on a subsistence farm—took one of the twins in his arms and walked the family to a Coast Guard vessel. Then he told the Senate something that should have changed everything.

Congress had passed the Goldwater-Nichols Act in 1986 to force the military branches to work together. Before that, the Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marines ran their own operations with catastrophic redundancy and catastrophic gaps. “You gave us Goldwater-Nichols, and it was a bitter pill to swallow,” Honoré testified. “You’ve got a joint, dependent, interagency, knock-’em-down Department of Defense. You don’t have that in the interagency.”

Nobody ever passed a Goldwater-Nichols for civilian disaster response.

MIT researchers Thomas Malone and Kevin Crowston spent the 1990s building the science of coordination. Their framework is elegant: coordination is managing dependencies between activities. When it fails, it’s not because people didn’t try. It’s because no mechanism existed to manage the dependency. The World Bank’s economists Hoff and Stiglitz reached the same conclusion from a different angle—coordination failure isn’t a motivation problem, it’s an infrastructure problem. Everyone would be better off acting together. They simply can’t.

And when the infrastructure does exist, the math changes dramatically. Network effects mean that every new participant in a coordination system exponentially increases its capacity. Complex systems researchers at Oxford found that through self-organization, coordination, and cooperation, a connected system realizes “exponential growth of capacity”—far beyond the simple sum of its parts. The twenty neighbors who built Stanley’s road would have been ten times more effective if they could see what every other group of neighbors in Yancey County was doing at the same time.

In 2017, Hurricane Harvey displaced 780,000 people in Houston. Fifty-four independent fire departments tried to coordinate with no shared picture. The same year, Hurricane Maria knocked out ninety-five percent of Puerto Rico’s cell towers. FEMA had 695 generators for 2,273 requests. In 2024, Helene hit the North Carolina mountains and WebEOC—the platform that’s supposed to track resources—crashed under ten times its normal volume. Two search-and-rescue systems operated with zero integration. Facebook became the missing persons database.

We tracked fourteen core structural problems across the after-action reports from Katrina through Helene. The number solved between 2005 and 2024 is zero.

The organizations doing this work—Red Cross, Emergency RV, Free Hot Meals, the VOADs coordinating dozens of member nonprofits, Salvation Army, faith-based groups, community action agencies—they are extraordinary. Emergency RV started with one person after the Camp Fire in Paradise, California in 2018. They’ve now delivered over 160 RVs to survivor families across the country, working with the state of Florida to coordinate five hundred donations through seven spreadsheets. These are not broken organizations. These are remarkable people doing remarkable work with tools that were never designed for what they’re being asked to do. They coordinate through text chains and Facebook posts and spreadsheets and phone calls to nineteen different providers. They do it because there is nothing else.

The coordinator at SBCVOAD in San Bernardino—a guy named Eric who’s been through floods firsthand—said it plainly: “Ideally it wouldn’t require them to download an app. You’re able to get basic information via text.” That’s what practitioners actually need. Not a platform. Not a dashboard. Infrastructure so simple it works on a text message.

• • •

Everyone who tried to build this before was too early. The technology wasn’t ready.

Two years ago, conversational AI couldn’t reliably extract structured information from someone texting “my roof collapsed, three kids, no car, no power.” It would hallucinate details, miss urgency signals, lose context between messages. You couldn’t trust it with people in crisis. That changed. Not gradually—in a single generation of models. Agentic AI—systems that don’t just answer questions but take actions, coordinate across parties, maintain context across hundreds of conversations—is a 2025 and 2026 development. It did not exist in any usable form when Honoré was testifying to the Senate. It barely existed when Helene hit.

And then in April 2026, Google released Gemma 4 under an open-source license. On-device AI that runs on a phone. No internet required. No cell signal. No infrastructure at all. A hundred and forty languages. Vision, audio, structured data extraction—all running locally on hardware that costs less than a hundred dollars.

Think about what that means for a disaster zone where ninety-five percent of cell towers are down. Think about what it means for a volunteer with a phone in a mountain hollow with no signal. Think about what it means for the six or seven days before anyone can reach Stanley and Judy. The tools that would have tracked FEMA’s bus promise. That would have deconflicted search and rescue so the same house doesn’t get checked four times. That would have connected the hay convoys across fourteen states to the drop sites that actually needed supplies instead of the ones that were already overflowing. That would have let Paul and Amy Dubuque find Dante and Raven in hours instead of weeks.

Those tools exist now. For the first time in the history of disaster response, the coordination layer is technically possible.

• • •

SOS started during Helene. Jonathan Greenberg’s farmhouse in Burnsville took bad damage. Insurance didn’t cover it—rodent infestation clause, the kind of thing you don’t think about until you’re standing in your kitchen watching the plumber shake his head. Emergency RV got him and his girlfriend a trailer to live in for three months. He scraped four thousand missing persons posts off Facebook because 211 was down. He made RV deliveries—drove from Florida to North Carolina. He brought Starlink terminals to people who’d been cut off for weeks. He saw what his neighbors did with the excavator and the gravel and the ATV trail, and he understood something that reframes everything about how disaster response should work.

There is no clean line between the people who need help and the people who give it. The same person who needs shelter today volunteers their generator tomorrow. A nurse triaging injuries on her front porch needs someone to watch her kids. The Dubuques donated their camper—and they’d been through losses of their own.

Everyone is a helper.

Everyone is a helper. That’s not a tagline. It’s a field observation—but it also turns out to be a neurological fact.

Helping others triggers the release of oxytocin, dopamine, and serotonin—the same chemicals behind a runner’s high. Neuroscientists call it the “helper’s high.” Research published in the Journal of Neuroscience in 2025 found that higher oxytocin receptor levels in the brain directly correlated with increased helping behavior—not because people were taught to help, but because the prosocial network is built into our neural architecture.

Helping isn’t learned behavior. It’s how we’re wired.

And it cascades. A study in Nature’s Scientific Reports found that cooperation through pay-it-forward reciprocity is driven by gratitude and empathy—positive emotions that are literally contagious through social networks. A single act of generosity inspires an average of three more along the chain. The instinct isn’t just there. It multiplies.

This is what Jonathan observed in the field after Helene. Needs and offers coexist on the same person, sometimes on the same day. The grandmother who received Starlink service was checking on her neighbor the next morning. The volunteers who cleared the ATV trail didn’t stop at their own road. The pay-it-forward instinct is already running at scale in every disaster. It just has no infrastructure to compound through.

Any coordination system that only sees one side of a person—helper or victim, never both—is leaving most of the available capacity on earth invisible.

SOS is community coordination infrastructure. Conversational AI agents replace multi-page government forms with two-minute natural language intake across SMS, WhatsApp, voice, and web. No app. No account. Any language. Any phone. People who need help and people who can offer it go through the same flow. A matching engine—inspired by the organ transplant network—scores connections across proximity, capability, urgency, and vulnerability. One family, three responders, connected simultaneously. Every match traced. Every outcome recorded.

Each partner organization gets its own AI agent, trained on their specific inventory, workflows, and terminology. The institutional knowledge that used to live in one overworked coordinator’s head—the knowledge that, in Bald Creek, volunteers had to reinvent from scratch by photographing food donations and counting servings by hand—now persists across every conversation and every event.

The revenue model matters as much as the technology. The disaster restoration market is forty-one billion dollars, and it’s rampant with fraud—over three billion in improper FEMA payments, a hundred and five thousand dollars per FEMA trailer versus five thousand for a donated RV coordinated through ERV. SOS builds trust scoring into the vendor layer. Revenue from connecting homeowners with vetted contractors funds coordination for people who have no insurance, no savings, no safety net. A 501(c)(3) that is always free for citizens and the organizations that serve them. A Public Benefit Corporation that generates revenue and reinvests it in the mission.

The coordination loop is running. Five partner organizations. Real three-way matching—survivor to resource to volunteer. During the Blizzard of 2026, SOS compiled 292 geocoded resources across nine states; Fox Weather covered the deployment. An Army veteran named Luke, who spent three weeks doing search and rescue during Helene, produced eighteen product ideas for SOS and is presenting it at the FEMA and DHS Irregular Warfare symposium in August. Twelve AI agents are in production today. The roadmap is one hundred partner agents, then a thousand community agents—each one trained on a specific neighborhood’s flood patterns, trusted organizations, volunteer networks. Then ten thousand communities.

What accumulates across all of those events is something that has never existed: a coordination science dataset. Not a database of needs and resources—those exist. A dataset of decisions. Which matches worked. Which failed and why. Which communities recovered fastest and what they had in common. Which resources run dry first. Which organizations deliver within the hour and which take a week. After enough events across enough communities, this becomes the empirical foundation for understanding how human coordination actually works in crisis—the kind of evidence base that could inform policy, funding, and infrastructure decisions for decades. Not because the data was extracted, but because it was earned through real coordination, from the people closest to the problem.

• • •

Twenty years of after-action reports say the same thing. The problems are known. The organizations doing the work are extraordinary. The response from ordinary people is massive and growing. And the infrastructure to connect all of it has never existed.

It’s being built now.

Disaster relief is where it starts. But the same coordination infrastructure could connect a citizen’s skills to a community need any day of the year. The same trust layer could hold contractors accountable year-round. The same feedback loop that surfaces post-disaster needs could give communities a real-time voice in how resources are allocated—not every four years, but continuously.

Jonathan said it to Paul on a Friday afternoon call: “Communities need tools to be able to surface information, match people with that information, give them the ability to weigh in and make changes on a community level.” That’s the longer vision. Not a disaster app. Community infrastructure.

It begins with coordination. It begins with disasters. It begins with earning the right to expand by proving it works where the need is most acute. Victoria and Jonathan and their team have spent eighteen months doing the research, talking to the people, building the technology, and learning from every disaster that hit while they were building. They’re not done. They’re getting started.

Everyone is a helper. The infrastructure to prove it is being built right now. And everyone who shows up is part of it.
