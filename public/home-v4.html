After Hurricane Helene hit western North Carolina in September 2024, FEMA's resource tracking system crashed under ten times its normal volume.³ Search and rescue teams checked the same homes three and four times because two platforms didn't talk to each other. Facebook became the missing persons database — not by design, but because nothing else worked.

This pattern isn't new. From Katrina to Sandy, Harvey to Maria, the same coordination failures surface every time. The tools break. The data fragments. The people who want to help can't find the people who need it.

Federal capacity is contracting

FEMA has stopped funding non-lifesaving disaster recovery. Twenty states have sued to restart the largest federal preparedness grant program.⁴ GAO added disaster assistance to its high-risk list for the first time.⁵

The technology now exists

AI coordination — conversational intake, real-time matching, multi-channel coordination — is feasible today. None of this was possible two years ago.

Coordination as a Force Multiplier

When it works, everything multiplies.

When coordination fails

A family needs shelter, food, and medical transport. Without coordination, two organizations never hear about the need. The third shows up a week late. The family called nine hotlines and filled out fifteen applications — to organizations that have no way of knowing what the others have already done. Nobody tracks what was spent. After Katrina, that absence of accountability cost billions in fraud.⁹ The system never learns from it.

When coordination fails — and today, it fails by default — the cost is measured in lives, in money lost to fraud, in communities that never fully recover, and in helpers who burn out because the system made them carry it alone.

When coordination works

The shelter organization, the food distribution team, and the medical transport volunteer all receive the same match at the same time. One family. Three partners. Connected simultaneously. Each knows what the other is doing. Every dollar is traced from source to outcome. Every contribution is attributed. The fraud surface shrinks because the data trail is auditable. And the system learns — which responses worked fastest, which resources ran dry, which partners delivered.

The technology to make this possible didn't exist two years ago. It does now.

Relief as Permaculture

The system learns. The network strengthens. The response accelerates.

Step 1

Intake

Text, QR, call, web. No app. No account. Works on any phone. People talk naturally — "my family needs a place to stay, we lost everything" — and the system extracts the need. People who want to help go through the same flow.

Step 2

Match

The system finds the closest people who can respond — capability, capacity, proximity, urgency. One family. Three partners. Coordinated simultaneously. Each gets the match through their existing tools.

Step 3

Coordinate

Each partner organization gets its own AI coordination agent — trained on their inventory, workflows, and rhythm. Context retained across every conversation, so each interaction builds on everything that came before.

Step 4

Fulfill

Every match is traced. Every dollar is tracked from source to outcome. Every contribution attributed — who helped, what was delivered, what it cost, whether it worked. The fraud surface shrinks because the data trail is auditable.

Step 5

Learn

After each cycle, the system knows which neighborhoods surge first, which partners respond within the hour, which resources run dry. That intelligence feeds the next disaster, and the one after that. The coordination compounds.

The system doesn't just respond to disasters — it learns from them. Every cycle feeds the next. The intelligence compounds. The network strengthens.

The system sustains itself. Relief as permaculture.

Revenue from connecting homeowners with vetted contractors funds coordination for people with no insurance, no savings, no safety net.

Born in a Hurricane

4,000 missing people on Facebook.
No system to find them.

SOS started during Hurricane Helene, when our team scraped 4,000 missing persons posts from Facebook because no system existed to track them. Our founder's farmhouse in Burnsville, North Carolina was damaged. He lived in a donated RV for three months. When roads washed out and isolated mountain communities from supplies, neighbors carved access trails through the terrain themselves. The team delivered RVs, brought Starlink terminals to people who'd been cut off for weeks, and started building the coordination tools that should have existed before the storm hit.

Since Helene, SOS has activated for every major disaster — including the Blizzard of 2026, when we coordinated hundreds of resources across 12 states.¹⁰ Fox Weather featured SOS during the storm, showing how the platform connects people to emergency resources in real time.

  • Partners keep their data. If they leave, it goes with them.
  • Citizens control their privacy. No profile required. Location data expires.
  • Every coordination decision is logged with reasoning. Full auditability.
  • SOS doesn't sell data, build profiles, or optimize for engagement.
  • Works for communities, not on them. That principle is in the architecture.
