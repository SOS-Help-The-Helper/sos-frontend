The SOS Story

Born in a hurricane.

SOS didn’t start in an office. It started in the mud, in the dark, in a mountain town the systems couldn’t reach.

Photo — Helene aftermath
Western North Carolina, September 2024

September 2024

When the systems went dark, the neighbors didn’t.

When Hurricane Helene hit western North Carolina, comms went down and missing-person reports scattered across spreadsheets and Facebook posts. Supplies piled up in the wrong places while neighbors went without. Our team scraped 4,000 missing-persons posts off Facebook because no system existed to track them.

Our founder’s farmhouse in Burnsville was damaged. He lived in a donated RV for three months. When roads washed out and isolated mountain communities from supplies, neighbors carved access trails through the terrain themselves. The team delivered RVs, brought Starlink terminals to people who’d been cut off for weeks, and started building the coordination tools that should have existed before the storm hit.

We discovered the instinct to help is always there. What’s missing is infrastructure.

Built on research

18 months studying how help fails.

After Helene, we spent eighteen months studying the history of disaster coordination and the science of coordination itself — from Katrina to Maria, from volunteer convergence to the $45 billion disaster-restoration economy that moves through communities and out again.

The founding team brings together experience in enterprise technology, humanitarian work, and field research. SOS lives at the intersection: the technology to coordinate at scale, and the conviction that coordination should serve people, not extract from them.

The values that came out of that work are structural, not aspirational. Partners keep their data — if they leave, it goes with them. Citizens control their privacy. No profile required. Location data expires. SOS doesn’t sell data, build profiles, or optimize for engagement.

Since Helene

Activated for every major disaster since.

Sept 2024
Hurricane Helene. SOS born in the field — 4,000 missing-persons posts tracked, RVs and Starlink delivered to Burnsville and surrounding communities.
2025
18 months of research into disaster-coordination history and coordination science. Architecture and the “everyone is a helper” model take shape.
Blizzard 2026
Coordinated hundreds of resources across 12 states — warming shelters, power-outage tracking, emergency supply distribution. Featured by Fox Weather during the storm.
Today
Production software. AI coordination agents live with partner organizations across housing, food, medical, supplies, and transport. The citizen portal is live. The coordination loop is running.

Where this goes

Disaster relief is where SOS starts.

The same coordination infrastructure could connect a citizen’s skills to a community need beyond disasters. The same trust layer could hold institutions accountable year-round. The same feedback loop could give communities a real-time voice in how resources are allocated — not every four years, but continuously.

Communities that have watched extraction happen for generations deserve infrastructure that works for them, not on them. That principle is in the architecture. It begins with coordination. It begins with disasters. It begins with earning the right to expand by proving it works where the need is most acute.
